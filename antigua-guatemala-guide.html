Orientation

What to know before you move

The essentials, up front: altitude that will humble your first run, cobblestones that will humble your tuk-tuk ride, money, and how people actually talk here. Antigua rewards slow pace and sharp awareness — which, coming off Bogotá at 2,640m, you already own the altitude half.

Currency

Guatemalan Quetzal

GTQ · symbol Q. USD widely accepted in tourist areas but you'll get better rates using quetzales for daily spending.

Exchange Rate

≈ Q 7.63

1 USD ≈ Q 7.60–7.65 (late June 2026). One of Central America's most stable currencies — has held the Q7.60–7.80 band since 2022. Divide by 7.6 for fast USD math.

Altitude

1,530 m

≈ 5,020 ft. Significantly lower than Bogotá's 2,640m — you'll actually breathe easier here. Still, first 48 hours: slow your pace ~8–10%, hydrate more than feels necessary. By day 3, your legs will feel unusually fast.

Getting Around

Tuk-tuks & private

Inside Antigua: tuk-tuks (auto-rickshaws) are the local move — Q10–20 for most hops. Beyond city limits: always private driver, never public bus. Uber coverage is limited here.

Running at 1,530 m

Adjust & Own It

Coming from Bogotá (2,640m), Antigua is lower — you may feel a surge of power. Cobblestones in the historic core require trail shoes or trail-aware road shoes. Best flat route: the Jocatenango loop (4–5 mi). Hills: Cerro de la Cruz. Trails: Acatenango foothills.

Navigation

Grid + Compass

Antigua runs on a colonial grid: Calles run E-W, Avenidas run N-S. Everything is tagged Norte/Sur/Oriente/Poniente from the Parque Central. It clicks within 20 minutes of walking.

Staying sharp — safety in Antigua

  • Antigua is Guatemala's safest destination for tourists — the historic core is walkable day and night on busy streets.
  • The main tourist zone (Parque Central, 5a Avenida Norte, Calle del Arco) has visible tourist police presence.
  • Stick to well-lit central streets after dark; less-trafficked alleys are not recommended late at night.
  • Tuk-tuks are safe for short hops; agree on the price before you get in (most rides are Q10–20 in-town).
  • For any trip outside Antigua — Lake Atitlán, Acatenango, Guatemala City — use a private driver, not public buses.
  • Less-frequented hike trails can attract opportunistic crime; use operators with security presence or police escort.
  • Don't drink tap water — bottled only. Restaurants serving tourists typically use purified water for cooking and ice.
  • ATMs: use machines inside banks or inside La Bodegona. Avoid street ATMs at night.
  • Tourist emergency: 1500 (INGUAT tourist assistance line). General emergency: 110 (police).
  • Passport safe in your room; carry a phone photo instead plus one card and modest cash.

A coffee region that eats seriously

Antigua sits inside one of Guatemala's five great coffee growing zones — volcanic soil, altitude, and consistent rainfall produce some of the world's most recognized single-origin beans. The same agricultural richness flows into the kitchen: fresh corn, black beans, local chiles, and an indigenous culinary tradition that's been developing for centuries. The best restaurants here cook with all of it.

03 · The Afternoons

One neat experience a day

Antigua is a walking museum — colonial ruins, jade culture, Indigenous textile arts, volcano sightlines, chocolate from its cacao origins. A different afternoon every day for two weeks is doable without repeating.

Signature

ChocoMuseo bean-to-bar workshop

4a Calle Poniente #8 · near Parque Central

Guatemalan cacao has indigenous roots going back thousands of years — this 2-hour hands-on workshop takes you through the full process: raw cacao to finished chocolate, with a Mayan ceremonial cacao drink at the end. Walk out with what you made. One of the top-rated experiences in the city for good reason.

Walk-in most days; book ahead for groups. Q200–280.

Textile arts

Backstrap weaving class, San Antonio Aguas Calientes

20 min from Antigua by shuttle or tuk-tuk

The women's weaving co-op in this small Maya-Kaqchikel village teaches the backstrap loom — the same technique used for centuries to produce the huipiles at Nim Po't. A genuinely immersive half-afternoon; bring Brooke. The village market is excellent for picking up pieces direct from the makers.

Ask at your accommodation for the current best co-op contact.

Architecture

Santa Catalina Arch & Calle del Arco golden hour

4a Calle Poniente & 5a Av Norte

The most iconic image in Guatemala — a colonial arch that once let cloistered nuns cross between convent buildings without being seen. At golden hour, with Volcán de Agua framed in the background, it's genuinely one of the most beautiful urban views in the Americas. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset.

Free. Crowds peak around sunset — arrive slightly early.

Colonial ruins

La Recolección & San Francisco ruins

Within walking distance of Centro

Antigua's ruins are UNESCO-protected and dramatically beautiful — roofless baroque churches frozen mid-collapse by the 1773 earthquake. La Recolección is the most photogenic (admission Q50); San Francisco has the tomb of the venerated Hermano Pedro. An eerie, gorgeous hour.

Cerro de la Cruz

Hilltop cross — dawn or dusk

North of Centro · 1.6 km from Parque Central

A stone-paved path climbs 121m to a giant iron cross above the city. At dawn you'll have it to yourself with the volcanoes clear. At dusk, city lights spread below you. Go with a guide or in a small group — the path is safe when others are present, but not for solo pre-dawn runs.

Municipal shuttles run from the Centro in the morning.

Coffee origin

Coffee finca tour — origin in your hands

Finca Filadelfia or La Azotea · 15 min from Centro

Finca Filadelfia is a working coffee estate on the slopes of Acatenango that offers guided tours through cultivation, wet-milling, drying, and cupping. La Azotea combines coffee and jade history in a single afternoon. If you're serious about coffee, seeing origin up close in Antigua is unmissable.

Book online or at the gate; Filadelfia has the most polished operation.

Jade culture

Casa del Jade museum & workshop

4a Calle Oriente

The Maya considered jade more precious than gold — Casa del Jade explains why, through artifacts, a jade workshop you can watch, and retail pieces of genuine quality. More cultural depth than a typical jewelry shop. Worth spending an hour even if you're not buying.

Museum entry Q30; pieces are investment-grade. Verify hours on arrival.

Market morning

Compañía de Jesús handicraft market

6a Av Norte · former Jesuit church

Set inside the ruins of a 17th-century Jesuit church — rows of textiles, jade, leather, and ceramic vendors under the remaining stone arches. The most atmospheric market in the city; far superior to the main tourist market. Best on Saturday mornings.

04 · The Weekend

Lake Atitlán — or Acatenango crater

Two completely different weekends depending on whether you want to run at altitude or summit a 3,976m volcano. Both are under 3 hours with a private driver. Both deliver views you won't forget. Here's how to plan each.

Always hire a private driver — no public buses

The Pan-American Highway to Lake Atitlán (76 km, ~2.5 hrs) is narrow, winding, and high-stakes. Guatemala's public buses ("chicken buses") are statistically dangerous and not for tourists. A round-trip private driver costs $60–100 and lets you control timing, make stops, and arrive in one piece. Never rent a car for this route unless you know the roads.

Option A — Lake Atitlán long-run weekend

Saturday AM Early departure from Antigua (7–8am). Arrive Panajachel by 10am. Lancha (water taxi) to San Marcos La Laguna or San Juan — the best base for trail access and village atmosphere.
Saturday afternoon Explore San Juan's women's weaving collective and lakefront paths. Dinner in San Marcos or back in Panajachel — Restaurante Circus is the top dining option near Panajachel.
Sunday — the long run (14–18 mi) Run the lakeside trail between villages: San Juan → San Pedro → Santiago (or back). Terrain is hilly volcanic trail — plan for ~1,500 ft of elevation. Lake Atitlán sits at 1,562m — nearly identical to Antigua, so no altitude adjustment needed. Lush, humid, spectacular lakeside views. Hydrate with sealed water only; carry more than you think you need. Have your driver or a local crew a turnaround point.
Sunday PM Post-run lunch in San Pedro or Panajachel, then the 2.5-hr drive back to Antigua. Home by 6–7pm.

Option B — Acatenango crater overnight (the harder, more epic call)

The setup Acatenango is 3,976m (13,045 ft) — one of the tallest volcanoes in Central America. Overnight at base camp (~3,600m) positions you directly across a saddle from Volcán de Fuego, which erupts every 15–30 minutes through the night. The best natural light show in the region.
Day 1 — ascent Depart Antigua 8am with a guiding operator. 4–5 hrs of steep ascending trail (1,500m vertical gain from the trailhead). Arrive base camp mid-afternoon. Watch Fuego erupt at sunset. Temperature drops to 3–5°C overnight — bring real layers.
Day 2 — summit & descent 3am wake-up, 1-hour summit push in the dark. Sunrise from 3,976m over a sea of clouds. Descend 3–4 hours, back in Antigua by early afternoon. This counts as your long run for the week — the descent alone is a serious trail effort.
Operators OX Expeditions and Trek Guatemala both run excellent overnight packages ($50–150). For Christopher's fitness level, this is very doable — but be honest with the guide about your ultra background so they pace you appropriately through the ascent.

Running note for both options: At 1,500–1,560m, you're at nearly identical altitude to Antigua — no major adjustment needed if you've already acclimatized here for the first few days. Acatenango's summit push at 3,976m is the exception — expect significant breathing difficulty in the final 400m vertical.

05 · Right Now

Tonight, and your first fancy dinner

Antigua is compact enough that nothing is more than 10 minutes away on foot or tuk-tuk. First night: easy and excellent. Second big night: the table that defines the trip.

Tonight · chill

Café No Sé or Sobremesa

1a Av Sur / Centro · 5 min walk

Café No Sé for mezcal, tacos, and the low-lit, reliably excellent Antigüeño bar atmosphere — walk-in friendly most nights. Or Sobremesa for natural wine and small plates in a quieter, more intimate room. Either is the right first-night energy: good food, no drama, properly landed.

Walk-in friendly before 8pm; after that it fills.

The marquee dinner · book immediately

Hector's Bistro

Centro · ~5 min walk

Twenty seats. Chef Hector Pérez. Rotating creative Guatemalan-European menu with no substitutions and no compromises. The best meal available in Antigua — and one of the finest in Central America. Book the moment you arrive; a week ahead is standard. If full, pivot to Mesón Panza Verde as the equally memorable alternative.

Reserve via WhatsApp or in person the day you arrive. They fill up.

The perfect Antigua morning-after

Early run — Jocatenango Loop The most runnable flat option from the Centro — a 4–5 mile loop through San Felipe and Jocatenango with rolling terrain and cobblestone-free stretches. Or head to the Cerro de la Cruz path for a climbing 5K that rewards with city views. Best before 7am to avoid foot traffic and the midday sun.
Recovery coffee Walk to Artista de Café or Alegría Café for your post-run pour-over. Both open by 7am; both are within 10 minutes of most Centro accommodations. Order the single-origin filter and ask what's newest in from the Antigua origin.
Mid-morning — Santa Catalina Arch The most iconic shot in Guatemala is 30 seconds from most hotels — worth seeing both in early morning light (fewer tourists, soft sun from the east) and at golden hour (Agua volcano backlit in the frame). Walk 5a Av Norte for the full Calle del Arco experience.
Afternoon — ChocoMuseo or a finca tour Day one sets the tone: ChocoMuseo if you want something hands-on and central; Finca Filadelfia if you want to see coffee at origin within 15 minutes of the city.
06 · Luxury Shopping

Jade, textiles & the best of Central American craft

Antigua doesn't have a luxury mall — it has something better: some of the world's finest indigenous textile work, pre-Columbian jade jewelry, artisan leather, and handwoven goods that you genuinely cannot find at home. This is where you invest in the real thing.

Essential · Textiles

Nim Po't Centro de Textiles Tradicionales

5a Av Norte #29

The single best textile destination in Guatemala — functioning simultaneously as retail space, cultural center, and museum. Curates museum-quality huipiles, antique pieces no longer produced, and contemporary work by identified master weavers. Prices are fixed (no bargaining) and reflect fair trade practice. Staff explain the specific village origin and symbolic meaning of each piece. If you buy one textile in Guatemala, buy it here.

Set prices, no haggling. Budget Q500–Q5,000+ for quality pieces.

Jade jewelry

Casa del Jade

4a Calle Oriente

The finest jade jewelry in Antigua — and the most culturally informed purchase you can make. Maya jade (jadeíte) was more valuable than gold; Casa del Jade's artisans work with this lineage consciously. Includes a museum tour explaining jade history and Mayan significance. The retail pieces are serious investment quality, with provenance.

Museum tour Q50 (credited toward purchase). Worth seeing even if you don't buy.

Jade market

Jades S.A.

4a Calle Oriente · near Casa del Jade

The competing jade house with an equally strong reputation and slightly broader range. Both Jades S.A. and Casa del Jade produce excellent work — visit both and compare. Their certification guarantees genuine Guatemalan jadeíte, not serpentine (the common imitation).

Handicraft market

Compañía de Jesús market

6a Av Norte · inside Jesuit ruins

Antigua's most atmospheric market, set inside the partially standing walls of a 17th-century Jesuit church. Leather goods, ceramics, woodwork, weavings — negotiate here, unlike Nim Po't. Best on Saturday mornings when vendors are most numerous and produce the freshest stock.

Bargaining is expected and good-natured. Starting counter-offer: 60% of asking price.

Artisan leather

La Casa del Cuero

Centro

Handmade Guatemalan leather goods — bags, belts, sandals, wallets — at a quality level that competes with European artisan work at a fraction of the cost. Custom work available with a few days' lead time. The belts and tote bags are particularly strong.

Custom orders: allow 3–5 days. Most items available immediately.

Concept boutique

Colección Casa

5a Av Norte

Curated home goods and lifestyle objects with strong Guatemalan design identity — ceramics, weavings, artisan soaps, decorative pieces. The most design-forward gift shop in the city; well-edited, beautiful packaging. For pieces that travel well and look intentional on a shelf back home.

08 · Stock the Kitchen

Groceries, markets & premium provisions

Antigua doesn't have a Whole Foods analog, but it has something close — fresh indigenous produce, an organic specialty store, and one of the liveliest daily markets in Central America. Here's where to shop for a proper cook-at-home week.

Main supermarket

La Bodegona

4a Calle Poniente #27

The primary full-service supermarket in Antigua — split into household goods and a conventional grocery with fresh produce, dairy, meat, and imported packaged goods. Not a luxury market, but the most complete option in the city for full weekly provisioning. The fresh produce here is serviceable; for premium fruit and vegetables, pair it with Mercado Central.

Main Antigua supermarket. In-town ATM inside is the safest cash machine.

Organic · premium

Orgánica

Centro

The only organic products supermarket in Antigua — natural and organic packaged goods, supplements, health foods, specialty teas, and some fresh organic produce. Smaller inventory than La Bodegona but significantly higher quality. The place to find certified organic coffee, local honey, chia, and imported health items. Your Whole Foods equivalent.

Premium pricing. Best for organic staples and specialty health items.

Fresh produce

Mercado Central

3a Calle Poniente · near Mercado de Artesanías

The local fresh market — the best produce in the city, bought directly from small farmers. Fruit, vegetables, herbs, dried chiles, beans, local cheese, fresh corn tortillas, and flowers. Busiest and freshest early morning (6–9am). The produce here is significantly better than La Bodegona's packaged equivalent. Bring a tote bag and cash (Q bills).

Cash only. Peak freshness 6–10am. Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday are the biggest market days.

Imported goods

La Torre (nearest branch)

Carretera a Ciudad Vieja · 10 min by tuk-tuk

Guatemala's premium supermarket chain — closest to Antigua. Carries the widest selection of imported products, specialty cheeses, wines, organic items, and international brands. If you need specific imported items (parmesan, quality wine, specialty olive oil) that La Bodegona doesn't carry, La Torre is worth the tuk-tuk ride.

10–15 min from Centro. Best for wine and specialty imports.

Saturday only

Saturday farmers' market

Various locations · ask locally

A small Saturday morning market (location varies; ask your accommodation) where local organic and small-scale farmers sell directly. Atypically good for sourcing heirloom corn varieties, local honey, artisan cheese, herbs, and fermented products. One of the best mornings you can have in Antigua.

Location changes seasonally — confirm the week before.

For long runs

Nutrition & fueling logistics

La Bodegona + Orgánica + online

La Bodegona stocks basic electrolyte drinks and energy bars (Gatorade, local brands). Orgánica carries chia, natural energy foods, and some imported sports nutrition. For gels and specific running nutrition not available locally, bring from home or order ahead. Banana and coconut water from Mercado Central are your best natural fueling options for long runs.
